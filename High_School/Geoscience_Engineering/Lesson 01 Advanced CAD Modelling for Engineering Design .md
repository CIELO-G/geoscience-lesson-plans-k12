# Lesson 1: Advanced CAD Modelling for Engineering Design

## Grade Level
High School Engineering / STEM (Grades 10–12)

## Duration
1–2 class periods

## Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:

- Understand how CAD software converts **2D sketches into 3D models**
- Explain the purpose of **loft, sweep, and revolve operations**
- Create complex engineering geometries using CAD features
- Apply CAD operations to solve **engineering design problems**
- Justify modeling decisions based on **design intent**

## Materials Needed

- CAD workstations with installed CAD software (Fusion 360, SolidWorks, Inventor, or similar)
- Projector for displaying images from the CAD modelling presentation
- Starter CAD files derived from the demonstration images
- Student design worksheets
- Example technical drawings for modeling practice

---

# Engage

## Brief Phenomenon / Demonstration

Display slides or images showing several completed CAD models including:

- A lofted frustum
- A centerline lofted curved handle
- A swept U-profile along a curved path
- A revolved vase

Ask students:

> **How do you think the software created each object from the 2D sketches you see?**

Prompt students with a quick challenge:

- Which **2D elements** produced each 3D shape?
  - Profiles
  - Paths
  - Centerlines
  - Axis of rotation

Encourage students to discuss possible sketch elements and operations that generated the objects.

### Time
10 minutes

### Purpose
Activate prior knowledge of **2D sketches and modeling concepts**, while showing real CAD outcomes that spark curiosity about loft, sweep, and revolve operations.

---

# Explore

## Hands-On Activity (Paired Stations or CAD Lab)

Students rotate through CAD stations to experiment with different modeling techniques.

### Station A – Loft Using Rail Guides

Students:

1. Create two planar sketches:
   - A circle
   - A rectangle
2. Place the sketches on different planes.
3. Add two rail lines connecting corresponding points.
4. Perform a **loft using rails**.

Students observe how the software transitions smoothly between shapes.

---

### Station B – Centerline Loft

Students:

1. Draw three elliptical profiles of different sizes.
2. Place them along a **spline centerline**.
3. Perform a **centerline loft**.

Students observe how the orientation and position of profiles follow the guiding centerline.

---

### Station C – Sweep

Students:

1. Create a **U-shaped profile**.
2. Sketch a **curved path**.
3. Perform a **sweep of the profile along the path**.

Then students repeat using:

- A **circular profile**
- A **guide rail**

Students compare how the guide rail changes the resulting geometry.

---

### Station D – Revolve

Students:

1. Sketch a **cross-sectional profile**.
2. Add a **vertical axis of rotation**.
3. Perform a **360° revolve operation**.

Students generate a symmetric object such as a **vase or knob**.

---

### Materials
- CAD workstations
- Starter files derived from example images
- Projector displaying reference visuals

### Time
30–40 minutes

### Teacher Role
- Circulate through stations
- Ask probing questions such as:

> What changed when you added a rail?  
> Why did the centerline change the loft’s path?

- Record student observations for class discussion.

# Explain

## Whole-Class Debrief

Invite student pairs to explain what they observed during the stations.

Use example screenshots from the presentation as visual anchors.

### Key Concepts

**Loft**

- Blends multiple profiles together
- Rails or centerlines guide how the surface transitions between shapes

**Centerline Loft**

- Uses a guiding curve that determines the orientation and path of intermediate profiles

**Sweep**

- Moves a profile along a path to generate geometry
- Can sweep:
  - A profile
  - A solid toolbody
- Guide rails may control shape deformation along the path

**Revolve**

- Rotates a profile around an axis
- Produces symmetric solids such as bottles, knobs, or shafts

---

## Key Teacher-Led Points

### Definitions

**Loft**
Smoothly transitions between different cross-sections.

**Sweep**
Moves a profile along a defined path.

**Revolve**
Rotates a profile around an axis to create radial symmetry.

---

### Design Intent: When to Use Each Tool

- **Loft:** Smooth transitions between different cross-sections
- **Sweep:** Constant cross-section along a path
- **Revolve:** Symmetrical rotational parts

---

### Addressing Misconceptions

**Does a loft always require rails?**  
No. Rails are optional guides. Centerline lofts instead use a central path.

**Does a sweep always maintain constant area?**  
Only if scaling or guide rails are not applied.

### Time
15–20 minutes

# Elaborate

## Application Tasks

Students apply what they learned through an engineering design challenge.

### Design Challenge

Students design a **small ergonomic handle or nozzle** that combines:

- A **revolve-based grip section**
- A **loft transition** between an elliptical connector and rectangular attachment
- A **swept internal channel or guide**

### Design Constraints

- Fixed overall length
- Two connection faces
- A specified cross-sectional area at one location

Students reference design inspiration from technical drawing examples provided by the instructor.

---

### Engineering Justification

Students produce a **one-page explanation** describing:

- Which CAD features they used
- Why each operation was appropriate for the design
- Screenshots of their CAD model

Students connect their modeling choices to example shapes shown in the presentation.

---

## Differentiation / Extensions

**Advanced Students**

- Use **area loft controls** to set section area targets
- Compare how area constraints modify geometry

**Support**

- Provide starter models with pre-made sketches
- Require only one modeling operation (for example, performing a sweep on a given curve)

### Time
1–2 class periods

---

# Evaluate

## Formative Assessment

### Exit Ticket

Students answer:

- Name one difference between **loft and sweep**
- Give one situation where **revolve** is the preferred operation

### Observation Checklist

During the activity the teacher observes:

- Correct use of **profiles**
- Proper selection of **paths**
- Understanding of **rails or centerlines**
- Correct use of **axis selection**

---

## Summative Assessment

### Design Challenge Rubric

Students are evaluated on:

- Correct application of at least **two CAD operations**
- Meeting specified **design constraints**
- Quality of geometry
- Written **engineering justification**

### Deliverables

Students submit:

- CAD file
- Three rendered views (including an **isometric view**)
- One-page design rationale explaining modeling decisions

---

## Self-Assessment

Students reflect on the question:

> **Which CAD operation improved my design most, and what would I change next time?**

---

## Teacher Feedback

Provide targeted feedback on:

- Feature selection
- CAD modeling technique
- Adherence to engineering constraints
